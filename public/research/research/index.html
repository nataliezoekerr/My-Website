Research

I’m a population ecologist interested in life history evolution and population dynamics of insects. Currently, I am a 5th-year PhD candidate in the Crone Lab at Tufts University. My research involves using population models to evaluate life history strategies and trade-offs in insects. More specifically, I am investigating the adaptive significance of worker size variation in bumble bee (Bombus vosnesenskii) colonies and how this variation contributes toward colony growth. I am also investigating life history trade-offs between the invasive exotic cabbage white butterfly (Pieris rapae) and its non-invasive native counterpart, the mustard white butterfly (Pieris oleracea) as well as the effects of a warming climate on the phenology of the mustard white.

In 2012, I graduated from the University of Queensland with a bachelor of Environmental Science with first class honours majoring in Natural Resources Science. After graduating, I was a research assistant in the Buckley Lab working on various projects on ecological economics and species distribution modelling as well as helping set up a field site for NutNet in Brisbane, Australia. In 2013, I was also a field assistant at Harvard Forest, MA working on plant and insect demography projects, which is where I discovered my love for entomology before pursuing a PhD at Tufts.
